About Me I am a dedicated and aspiring aerospace engineer, with a master's degree from the University of Texas-Arlington and a robust background in mechanical engineering from Swami Keshwanand Institute of Technology, India. My academic journey has endowed me with a deep blend of theoretical knowledge and hands-on expertise. I am deeply passionate about innovation and am committed to achieving excellence in all my endeavors.
My professional experience includes working as a research assistant at the University of Texas at Arlington, where I specialized in advanced thermal cooling solutions, focusing particularly on metal foam single-phase liquid immersion cooling. Before this role, I contributed as an associate Mechanical engineer at AHA 3D Innovations Pvt. Ltd. And worked on STLA Brain as an Embedded systems Engineer at KPIT Technologies Ltd. I gained significant practical experience during my Mechanical Engineering internship at Bharat-Benz Pvt. Ltd. Notably, my projects such as the Aluminium Porous Foam Heat Transfer Study, the development of the Tesla Valvular conduit (Tesla valve), UAV System design, and Rocket Nozzle Design, highlight my capability to innovate and drive results in complex engineering challenges.

This work presented an experimental study on the thermal performance of aluminum foam heat sinks in single-phase liquid immersion cooling (SPLIC). A set of heat sinks with varying pores per inch (PPI) and height is employed to test the thermal properties of heat sinks under different flow rates and power. Metal foams are used in place of conventional heat sinks for cooling, particularly aluminum alloy (Al-6101-T6) foam, which is a type of lightweight porous material with a cellular structure consisting of a large volume fraction of gas-filled open pores. It has a higher surface area-to-volume ratio, higher thermal conductivity, is lightweight, has increased turbulence, and has structural integrity at high temperatures. The goal of this experiment is to analyze the thermal and fluid properties of an aluminum foam heat sink when it is immersed in a synthetic dielectric fluid (EC-100). A customized tank was designed and fabricated for the experimental setup with a Thermal Test Vehicle (TTV), enabling the control of flow rate, heater temperature, and inlet temperature of the fluid. A set of experiments were conducted using aluminum foam heat sinks with varying pores per inch (5, 10, 20, and 40 PPI) for varying flow rates (1, 2, and 3 LPM) and heights (0.5 and 0.75 inches) with a relative density of 10~12%, mounted on a heater plate with heat fluxes of 19 W/cm^2 and 23.8 W/cm^2 respectively. Major outcomes from the experiment were that the heat sink of 10 PPI exhibited the best heat transfer and lowest thermal resistance, while the heat sink of 5 PPI exhibited the highest “overall surface area efficiency” in all conditions. The heat transfer was increased from 0.5-inch-high to 0.75-inch-hight foam cores.
